What is AQI and how is it calculated?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a single number used to communicate how polluted the air currently is, and what associated health effects might be a concern for the public. Instead of presenting raw pollutant concentrations for a handful of chemicals, AQI converts those numbers into a scale that is easier to understand.

Which pollutants are included?

Most AQI systems evaluate several common air pollutants. In India these typically include:

• PM2.5 (fine particulate matter) • PM10 (coarse particulate matter) • NO2 (nitrogen dioxide) • SO2 (sulphur dioxide) • O3 (ozone) • CO (carbon monoxide)

How is the AQI number produced?

Each pollutant's measured concentration is converted into a normalized sub-index using a breakpoint table (a set of ranges). The highest sub-index among the measured pollutants becomes the reported AQI. This approach ensures the AQI reflects the pollutant that poses the greatest health risk at the time.

What do the AQI categories mean?

Although the exact labels may vary by country, AQI categories generally indicate health risk growing from 'Good' to 'Hazardous'. For example:

0–50: Good 51–100: Satisfactory/Moderate 101–200: Poor 201–300: Very Poor 301–400: Severe 401–500: Hazardous

Limitations and context

AQI simplifies complex science into a single number; it is useful for public guidance, but not a substitute for detailed scientific analysis. AQI can vary rapidly over short distances and times; local sources and microclimates affect readings. Always consult local monitoring agencies for official advisories.

Practical takeaway

Use AQI to plan outdoor activities — when AQI is high, reduce strenuous outdoor activity, keep children and older adults indoors when possible, and follow local public health advice.
